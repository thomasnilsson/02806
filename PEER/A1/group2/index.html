This is Quantifiably the Best Month to go to the Farmers Market

As part of a homework assignment, I ask my students at Pratt to go out and chart something of interest to them, and Katherine Savarese came back with a simple chart about farmers markets that I loved- it inspired this post.

You probably know that farmers markets are a staple across all five boroughs of New York City, but September happens to be a very special month. Why is that? Well, it turns out September is the month with the most unique types of fresh produce- forty three to be exact.

To show the current offerings, we charted how many types of fruits and vegetables are available by month, and showed if they were fresh or from storage.

If you miss the month of September, you might be behind the fruit curve, but there are plenty more months of vegetables left. The chart shows that fresh vegetables are available 9 out of 12 months, but fruit is only available 5 out of 12. Now if only we could quantify how delicious the produce is…

Part 1:

  • The point of a jitter plot is to prevent the issue poor dot plots resulting from data points being on top of each other. When the points are not jittered it can be hard to tell how many points are really at a location,
  • Figure 2-3 shows that bins width isn't the only important consideration regarding bins; edge alignment can be equally impactful.
  • KDEs can be misleading for two main reasons. The first is that they can make relatively few data points appear to be a very complete representation of the distribution, when it is better to be honest about the data. The second is that due to different density functions and bandwidths, the same data can produce many different KDEs in less clear ways than with a histogram varying bin width and alignment, which again can be misleading.
  • CDFs have less intuitive appeal than histograms because the value associated a possible data value is not the amount of data points at that value, but the fraction of data points less than or equal to or greater than or equal to than point.
  • A quantile plot is set up so that the x-axis is percentages and the y-axis is the data values. This make sit more intuitive to look up what data value corresponds to a percentile.
  • Summary statistics are only applicable to unimodal and relatively normal distributions. Visualizing the full data is always better, as otherwise you could be given summary statistics for data where such statistics are not applicable and have no way of knowing how inaccurate they may be.
  • Box plots are most useful when comparing unimodal distributions. Box plots allow for a concise illustration of the data's structure, including its center, width, symmetry, tails, and outliers.
  • Violin plots are better than box plots for distributions that are not unimodal.

Part 2:

  • The argument for the data from Fig 3-1 has a small amount of noise is that the data points all appear to lie on the curve of a function, even if it is a very complicated function. If there was random noise, it would be hard to clearly see the curve of the function as you can in Fig 3-1.
  • A real world example of a multivariate relationship like the lower right panel of Fig 3-3 would be time vs. AC and heating expenses. The costs would be similar during seasons like fall and spring, but during the following seasons the costs would diverge as the costs in a place like Florida in the winter would go down, as little is needed, but up drastically in New York where heating is needed.
  • Janert mentions weighted splines and LOESS to find a smooth approximation of noisy data. A low-pass filter could also be used to produce a smoother subset of points.
  • Residuals are the difference between the smoothed trend and the data. It is a good idea to plot the residual to your fit because this allows you to see trends in the residuals that indicate problems with the fit. This includes trends and balance around zero.
  • About half the residuals should be above and below zero if it is a good fit line. By fitting a line to the data above and below the original fit line, we can say about half the data lies between these outer fit lines, as long as they are a good fit.
  • Semi log plots help you see exponential relationships and relative changes.
  • Loglog plots help you see power-law relationships as straight lines.
  • Banking tries to make the slopes of the curves as close as possible to 45 degrees by changing the aspect ration of plots. Changing the aspect ration can lead to a large loss in detail on one axis and make the graph harder for humans to perceive.
  • The linear fit for x on y will be different than for y on x, which means that the line is not a best fit to the points. Instead it is a function that predicts y in terms of x.
  • There are two types of graphs, those for graphical analysis and those for presentation. Graphs made for graphical analysis should be focused primarily on speed and ease without concern for polish like labels. Their purpose is to allow you to look at the data in many different ways. Presentation graphics should focus on polish and explanations via added elements, especially text. Overall, it is harder to speak to what makes a good presentation graphic as they are more a matter of taste, so it is important to look at graphs made by others to try to find what makes for an effective style.
